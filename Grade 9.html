Dynamic Electricity

Dynamic electricity occurs when electric charges move through a material, typically electrons traveling through a conductor like a metal wire. This movement of electrons, called electric current, transfers electrical energy from the power source to devices.

Dynamic electricity powers everyday technologies such as lamps, fans, and televisions. A complete path for electricity to move is called an electric circuit, which includes:

  • A power source (e.g., battery or generator)
  • Conducting wires
  • Electrical components (e.g., lamp, motor, resistor)
  • A switch to control the flow of electricity

When the circuit is closed, electrons move freely, and electricity flows. When the circuit is open, the current stops.

Conductors and Insulators

Conductors

Conductors are materials that allow electric current to flow easily because they contain many free electrons that can move freely.

Common conductors include:

  • Copper
  • Aluminum
  • Iron
  • Silver

Copper is widely used in electrical wiring because it is a very good conductor and relatively affordable.

Insulators

Insulators are materials that do not allow electric current to flow easily. Their electrons are tightly bound to atoms and cannot move freely.

Examples of insulators include:

  • Rubber
  • Plastic
  • Glass
  • Dry wood

Insulators are important for safety because they are used to cover electrical wires and prevent electric shock.

Electric current is measured using an ammeter, which is connected in series with the component being measured.

Higher current usually means more electrical energy is being transferred.

Electrical Resistance

Electrical resistance is the property of a material that slows down or opposes the flow of electric current.

Effect of resistance on electric current:

  • Materials with low resistance allow current to flow easily.
  • Materials with high resistance make it harder for current to pass.

Resistance is measured in ohms (Ω).

Electric Circuits

An electric circuit is a closed path that allows electric current to flow from a power source through different components.

There are two common types of circuits:

Series Circuit

Hover to see the explanation

A series circuit connects components in a single path. Electric current flows through each component one after another.

Characteristics:
• Same current flows through all components
• Voltage is shared between components
• Total resistance increases when more devices are added

If one component breaks, the entire circuit stops working.
Example: old Christmas lights.

Parallel Circuit

Hover to see the explanation

A parallel circuit has multiple branches for current to flow.

Characteristics:
• Each component receives the same voltage
• Current splits between different branches
• Total resistance decreases when more branches are added

If one component stops working, the others still operate.
Example: household electrical wiring.
