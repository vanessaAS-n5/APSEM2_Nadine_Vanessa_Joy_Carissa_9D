Physics • Thermal Expansion

GRADE 8

Learn how thermal expansion works, why materials expand when heated, and how this concept is used in real-life science and engineering.

Introduction to Physics Concepts

Thermal Expansion

Thermal expansion is the tendency of a substance to change its size when the temperature changes. Most materials expand when heated and contract when cooled. This occurs because all matter is made of tiny particles that are always moving.

When a material is heated, the particles gain kinetic energy and move faster. This increased motion causes the particles to spread farther apart, increasing the size of the object. When temperature decreases, particle motion slows and the particles move closer together, causing the object to contract.

Thermal expansion can affect the length, area, or volume of a material. Engineers must consider thermal expansion when designing bridges, railway tracks, buildings, and pipelines to prevent structural damage.

Why Thermal Expansion Happens

All matter is made of extremely tiny particles such as atoms and molecules.
These particles are constantly moving and vibrating.
Heating gives particles more energy, making them move faster.
Faster particle motion increases the distance between particles.
Cooling reduces particle motion and decreases the space between particles.
The particles themselves do not grow larger — only the space between them changes.

Thermal Expansion in Different States of Matter

Solids

Solids expand the least because their particles are tightly packed and strongly bonded. Expansion mainly occurs as an increase in length or volume.
Liquids

Liquids expand more than solids because their particles have weaker bonds and can move more freely. This property is used in thermometers.
Gases

Gases expand the most since their particles are very far apart and move freely in all directions. Gas volume can increase greatly when heated.

Linear Expansion in Solids

Linear expansion refers to the change in length of a solid material when the temperature changes. Different materials expand by different amounts when heated.

ΔL = L₀ × α × ΔT

Change in Length = Original Length × Coefficient of Expansion × Temperature Change

ΔL
Change in length of the object
L₀
Original length of the object
α
Coefficient of linear expansion (depends on the material)
ΔT
Change in temperature

Materials such as aluminum expand more than steel because they have a larger coefficient of expansion.

Thermal Expansion in Gases

Gas particles move freely and very quickly. When a gas is heated, the particles move faster and collide more frequently with the walls of the container.

If the container can expand, the gas volume increases. If the container is rigid and cannot change size, the pressure increases instead.

P₁ / T₁ = P₂ / T₂

Gay-Lussac's Law

P₁
Initial Pressure
P₂
Final Pressure
T₁
Initial Temperature (Kelvin)
T₂
Final Temperature (Kelvin)

This law states that pressure is directly proportional to temperature when the volume remains constant.

Thermal Expansion in Liquids

Liquids generally expand when heated and contract when cooled. However, water behaves differently between 0°C and 4°C. This unusual behavior is called Anomalous Expansion of Water.

Water expands when cooled from 4°C to 0°C.
Water contracts when heated from 0°C to 4°C.
Water reaches its maximum density at 4°C.
Because ice is less dense than water, it floats on the surface.

This property is extremely important for life on Earth. When lakes freeze, the ice forms on the surface instead of the bottom, allowing aquatic animals and plants to survive during winter.

Real Life Examples of Thermal Expansion

Railway tracks have small gaps to allow expansion during hot weather.
Bridges use expansion joints so they do not crack when temperatures change.
Electric power lines sag during hot weather due to expansion.
Thermometers use liquid expansion to measure temperature.
Metal lids on jars loosen when heated because the lid expands.
Hot air balloons rise because heated air expands and becomes less dense.
