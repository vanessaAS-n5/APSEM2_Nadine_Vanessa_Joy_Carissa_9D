Physics • Magnetism

GRADE 9 - SEMESTER 2

Learn how magnetism works, explore magnetic fields, and understand how electricity and magnetism are connected in real-world applications.

What is Magnet?

A magnet is an object that produces an invisible force called a magnetic field, which allows it to attract certain materials.

Types of Magnets

Magnets can be classified into soft and hard types. Soft magnetic materials, such as soft iron or ordinary steel, act as temporary magnets that magnetize easily but quickly lose their magnetism when the external magnetic field is removed. In contrast, hard magnetic materials, like neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB), alnico, and samarium-cobalt (SmCo), are permanent magnets that keep their magnetism for a long time and are not easily demagnetized.

Magnetic Poles

Every magnet has two poles: North and South. Opposite poles attract each other, while like poles repel.

Magnetic and Non-magnetic Material

Materials such as iron, nickel, cobalt, and steel are magnetic, while materials like plastic, wood, paper, aluminium, copper, and rubber are non-magnetic.

Magnetic Field

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where magnetic forces act. Although it cannot be seen directly, it can be shown using iron filings, which form curved lines around the magnet.

The strength of a magnetic field depends on the spacing of the field lines:

  • Field lines close together indicate a strong magnetic field
  • Field lines farther apart indicate a weak magnetic field

Field line move from the north pole to the south pole outside the magnet. The most dense part of the magnetic field is near the poles of a magnet, indicating that the poles is the strongest part of a magnet.

Making and Removing Magnets

Making Magnets

Magnets can be made through magnetization by rubbing a magnetic material with a magnet in one direction repeatedly. This aligns the magnetic domains inside the material.

Removing Magnets

Magnetism can be removed through demagnetization, usually by heating soft magnetic materials. Heat disrupts the alignment of magnetic domains, causing the magnetism to disappear. Other than heat, strong impacts such as hammering or dropping a magnet can also can disrupt the orientation of magnetic domains.

Electromagnetism: Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

Electromagnetism occurs when an electric current flows through a wire, producing a magnetic field around the wire. This magnetic field forms circular field lines surrounding the current-carrying wire.

How to increase the strength of electromagnet:

  • Increase the current
  • Increase the number of coils (turns)
  • Use a high-permeability ferromagnetic core such as a soft iron core

Direction of Magnetic Field

The direction of the magnetic field depends on the direction of the electric current.

Right-Hand Grip Rule

The Right-Hand Grip Rule is used to determine magnetic field direction:
  • The thumb points in the direction of the current
  • The curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field

Dot and Cross Symbols

  • A dot (•) represents current flowing out of the page, toward the observer
  • A cross (×) represents current flowing into the page, away from the observer

When the direction of the current is reversed, the direction of the magnetic field is also reversed.
