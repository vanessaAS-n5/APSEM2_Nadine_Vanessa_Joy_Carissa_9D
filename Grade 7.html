What is Force?

Force is a push or pull acting on an object due to interaction with another object. Whenever two objects interact, a force is applied. Forces are important in physics because they explain how objects move and change motion.

Forces can:

Change the speed of an object
Change the direction of motion
Start or stop movement
Cause acceleration
Change the shape of an object

The standard unit used to measure force is called the Newton (N). This unit is named after the scientist Isaac Newton, who studied motion and forces.

Newton's Laws of Motion

Newton’s Laws of Motion describe how forces affect the movement of objects. These three laws are the foundation of classical physics.

Newton's Law 1
An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion continues moving unless acted upon by an unbalanced force. This is called inertia.
Newton's Law 2
Acceleration depends on force and mass. More force produces greater acceleration, while larger mass makes acceleration harder.
Newton's Law 3
For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Example: when you jump, the ground pushes you upward.

Force Diagram

A force diagram, also called a free-body diagram, is used to show all the forces acting on an object. The object is represented by a dot or box, and arrows are used to represent forces.

The direction of the arrow shows the direction of the force, and the length of the arrow represents the strength of the force. Force diagrams help scientists and engineers understand how different forces affect motion.

Types of Forces

Forces can be divided into two main categories: contact forces and non-contact forces.

Friction

A force that resists motion when two surfaces rub together.
Gravity

The force that pulls objects toward the Earth.
Magnetic Force

The attraction or repulsion between magnets and magnetic materials.
Tension

The pulling force transmitted through a rope or string.
Normal Force

The support force exerted by a surface.
Air Resistance

A force caused by air pushing against a moving object.

Force Formula

F = m × a

Force = Mass × Acceleration

F = Force (Newton)
m = Mass (kg)
a = Acceleration (m/s²)

This formula shows that increasing the force will increase acceleration. However, objects with larger mass require more force to accelerate.

Examples of Force in Daily Life

Pushing a shopping cart in a supermarket
Kicking a soccer ball across a field
A magnet attracting metal objects
Gravity pulling a falling apple toward the ground
A parachute slowing down a skydiver
A person pulling a suitcase with wheels
